1. **Safety and Immunogenicity of a Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2 Spike Subunit Vaccine Stabilized in the Prefusion Conformation by a Second-Generation Molecular Clamp and Evaluated in Adults Aged 18-55 Years: A Randomized, Double-Blind, Active Comparator, Phase I Trial.**

   Journal group: infectious_diseases_microbiology_ipc  
   Translation horizon: >12 months  
   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41285172/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41285172/)  
   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf568](https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf568)  
   Type: Journal Article, Clinical Trial, Phase I, Randomized Controlled Trial  
   Why it matters:  
   - A new SARS-CoV-2 spike subunit vaccine (UQSC2) using a second-generation molecular clamp (MC2) was developed to avoid interference with HIV-1 diagnostic tests.  
   - In a phase I randomized, double-blind trial (n=70), UQSC2 was evaluated as a booster in adults previously vaccinated with mRNA vaccines.  
   - UQSC2 was as well tolerated as the active comparator NVX-CoV2373 (Novavax) and elicited comparable increases in neutralizing humoral responses against the Wuhan strain.  
   Headline result: The UQSC2 vaccine was as well tolerated as the authorized comparator NVX-CoV2373 and produced comparable increases in neutralizing humoral responses against the prototypic Wuhan strain of SARS-CoV-2.  
   Trial n: n=70 (35 per group)  
   Clinical takeaway:  
   - UQSC2, a novel SARS-CoV-2 vaccine candidate, demonstrated a favorable safety profile and immunogenicity comparable to Novavax's Nuvaxovid as a booster dose.  
   - The second-generation molecular clamp addresses a previous issue with HIV-1 diagnostic interference, potentially making this platform more viable for future vaccine development.  
   - These early phase I results support further clinical development to assess efficacy, broader variant coverage, and long-term safety.

2. **RSV Vaccination in Solid Organ Transplant Recipients: Interim Findings From a Phase 3 Trial of mRNA-1345.**  
   Journal: Clin Infect Dis | Date: 2026 | Score: 15 (rule 12, llm +3)  
   Journal group: infectious_diseases_microbiology_ipc  
   Translation horizon: >12 months  
   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41711453/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41711453/)  
   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciag108](https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciag108)  
   Type: Journal Article  
   Why it matters:  
   - Solid organ transplant (SOT) recipients are at increased risk for severe RSV disease and lack specific vaccine options.  
   - This interim phase 3 trial (n=150) found the mRNA-1345 RSV vaccine to be well tolerated and immunogenic in SOT recipients.  
   - A single dose induced significant neutralizing antibody responses (4.9-fold for RSV-A, 3.4-fold for RSV-B by Day 29), with modest additional increases after a second dose.  
   Headline result: The mRNA-1345 RSV vaccine was well tolerated and induced robust neutralizing antibody responses in solid organ transplant recipients, with a 4.9-fold increase in RSV-A and 3.4-fold increase in RSV-B nAb GMTs by Day 29 after one dose.  
   Trial n: n=150  
   Clinical takeaway:  
   - The mRNA-1345 RSV vaccine shows promise for preventing RSV in immunocompromised SOT recipients, a population with high unmet need.  
   - A single dose appears to be immunogenic, with a second dose potentially offering additional benefit, especially in specific subgroups like kidney/lung transplant recipients.  
   - The vaccine was well tolerated with no reported vaccine-related serious adverse events or transplant rejection.  
   Why prioritised: journal_weight=2; group_bias=3; article_type_heuristic:clinical trial, phase iii=+4; near_term_keywords=+1; id_keywords=+1; basic_science_keywords=+1; llm_priority=+3  

3. **Safety and Immunogenicity of a Klebsiella pneumoniae Tetravalent Bioconjugate Vaccine (Kleb4V) Administered to Healthy Adults: A First-in-Human Phase I/II Randomized and Controlled Study.**  
   Journal: J Infect Dis | Date: 2026 | Score: 14 (rule 12, llm +2)  
   Journal group: infectious_diseases_microbiology_ipc  
   Translation horizon: >12 months  
   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41289032/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41289032/)  
   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf600](https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf600)  
   Type: Journal Article, Clinical Trial, Phase I, Randomized Controlled Trial  
   Why it matters:  
   - Safe and effective vaccines are urgently needed to prevent Klebsiella pneumoniae infections, which this study addresses.  
   - Kleb4V is the first K. pneumoniae conjugate vaccine candidate to reach clinical assessment, marking a significant step in vaccine development.  
   - In this Phase I/II trial (n=166), Kleb4V was well tolerated and immunogenic for all four vaccine-serotypes, with AS03-adjuvanted formulations showing superior immune responses for three of four serotypes.  
   Headline result: The tetravalent bioconjugate vaccine Kleb4V was well tolerated and immunogenic in healthy adults aged 18-70 years, with AS03-adjuvanted formulations showing superior immune responses for O1v1, O2a, and O2afg serotypes.  
   Trial n: n=166  
   Clinical takeaway:  
   - Kleb4V is a promising first-in-human K. pneumoniae conjugate vaccine candidate, demonstrating good safety and immunogenicity profiles.  
   - The use of AS03 adjuvant significantly enhanced immune responses for most serotypes, suggesting its potential benefit in future formulations.  
   - Further research is needed to optimize the O3b response and advance this vaccine candidate through later-phase clinical trials.

   Type: Journal Article  
   Why it matters:  
   - Randomized trials of long-acting injectable antiretroviral therapy (ART) in persons with HIV who face challenges with adherence to oral medication were previously lacking.

   Trial n: n=306 (randomized in step 2)

   - Long-acting injectable cabotegravir-rilpivirine offers a highly effective treatment option for persons with HIV who struggle with adherence to daily oral ART.  
   - This regimen can significantly improve virologic outcomes in a population historically challenging to treat effectively.  
   - The study reinforces the importance of initial adherence support and incentives to achieve viral suppression before transitioning to long-acting injectables.

   - ST11-KL25 and ST11-KL64 are the predominant hv-CRKP subtypes in China, each with distinct epidemiological and phenotypic characteristics.  
   - ST11-KL64 demonstrates broader resistance, including to ceftazidime-avibactam and polymyxin, and higher in vivo virulence.  
   - ST11-KL25 appears to be a stable, colonization-adapted subtype with consistent resistance and moderate virulence, suggesting different adaptation strategies.

6. **"Should I stay or should I go"-a kinase delays escape of**  
   Journal: mBio | Date: 2026 | Score: 11 (rule 9, llm +2)  
   Journal group: infectious_diseases_microbiology_ipc  
   Translation horizon: >12 months  
   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41718479/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41718479/)  
   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1128/mbio.03885-25](https://doi.org/10.1128/mbio.03885-25)  
   Type: Journal Article  
   Why it matters:  
   - Candida glabrata, responsible for 15%-25% of invasive candidiasis, actively delays its escape from macrophages, with host cells bursting after 2-3 days.  
   - The fungal Ksp1 kinase was identified as a key regulator of this delay; its deletion accelerates macrophage cell lysis.  
   - Loss of Ksp1 increases mitophagy and the formation of petites, a respiration-deficient phenotype associated with resistance to antifungals and phagocytic killing.  
   - Deletion of KSP1 enhances resistance to multiple antifungals, suggesting a broader cross-adaptive survival strategy.  
   - This prolonged intramacrophagal phase may contribute to immune evasion, antifungal resistance, and potential recurrence of infection.  
   Headline result: The fungal Ksp1 kinase delays Candida glabrata's escape from macrophages, with its deletion accelerating host cell lysis and increasing antifungal resistance.  
   Trial n: not reported  
   Clinical takeaway:  
   - Understanding the mechanisms of C. glabrata's intracellular survival and delayed escape could inform new therapeutic strategies for persistent or recurrent candidiasis.  
   - Targeting fungal kinases like Ksp1 or pathways related to petite formation might offer novel approaches to overcome antifungal resistance and improve immune clearance.  
   Why prioritised: journal_weight=1; group_bias=3; near_term_keywords=+1; id_keywords=+2; basic_science_keywords=+2; llm_priority=+2  

7. **Corticosteroid Therapy and Long-Term Outcomes of Post-Infectious Inflammatory Syndrome in Non-HIV Immunosuppressed Cryptococcal Meningitis: A Multicenter Case Series.**  
   Journal: J Infect Dis | Date: 2026 | Score: 11 (rule 9, llm +2)  
   Journal group: infectious_diseases_microbiology_ipc  
   Translation horizon: >12 months  
   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41486473/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41486473/)  
   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf620](https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf620)  
   Type: Journal Article, Multicenter Study  
   Why it matters:  
   - Post-infectious inflammatory response syndrome (PIIRS) is a recognized cause of neurologic deterioration in cryptococcal meningoencephalitis (CM).  
   - This multicenter case series included 13 non-HIV immunosuppressed patients with CM who subsequently developed PIIRS.  
   - Corticosteroid therapy was associated with significant improvements in Karnofsky scores at 1 month (P = .001), with sustained benefit at 6 and 12 months (P = .002).  
   - All 10 surviving patients (out of 13) demonstrated resolution of neurological symptoms after corticosteroid therapy.  
   - CSF inflammatory parameters and brain MRI findings also showed significant improvement with corticosteroid use.  
   Headline result: Corticosteroid therapy significantly improved Karnofsky performance scores at 1 month (P = .001), with sustained benefit at 6 and 12 months (P = .002), and resolved neurological symptoms in all 10 surviving non-HIV immunosuppressed patients with cryptococcal meningitis-associated PIIRS.  
   Trial n: n=13  
   Clinical takeaway:  
   - Corticosteroids may be a beneficial treatment option for non-HIV immunosuppressed patients experiencing PIIRS secondary to cryptococcal meningitis, improving neurological and CSF outcomes.  
   - Clinicians should be aware of potential adverse events, such as sepsis, when considering corticosteroid therapy in this immunocompromised patient group.  
   Why prioritised: journal_weight=1; group_bias=3; near_term_keywords=+1; id_keywords=+4; llm_priority=+2
