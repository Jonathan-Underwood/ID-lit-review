1. **Safety and Immunogenicity of a Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2 Spike Subunit Vaccine Stabilized in the Prefusion Conformation by a Second-Generation Molecular Clamp and Evaluated in Adults Aged 18-55 Years: A Randomized, Double-Blind, Active Comparator, Phase I Trial.**

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41285172/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41285172/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf568](https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf568)
   Type: Journal Article, Clinical Trial, Phase I, Randomized Controlled Trial

   **Trial n:** n=70 (35 per group)

   **Why it matters:**
   - Ongoing development of COVID-19 vaccines is crucial for addressing emerging variants and improving vaccine platforms.
   - Previous molecular clamp vaccine designs faced issues with HIV diagnostic interference, necessitating the development of safer alternatives.
   - A new molecular clamp technology could enable rapid and versatile vaccine development against various respiratory viruses, enhancing pandemic preparedness.

   **Headline result:** The UQSC2 vaccine, utilizing a second-generation molecular clamp, was as well tolerated as the NVX-CoV2373 comparator and elicited comparable increases in neutralizing humoral responses against the prototypic Wuhan strain of SARS-CoV-2.

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - This Phase 1 data suggests the MC2 platform is safe and immunogenic, but further larger trials are needed to confirm efficacy and broader variant coverage.
   - The primary limitation is the small sample size and focus on a single booster dose in previously vaccinated individuals, limiting generalizability to primary vaccination or other populations.
   - Watch for future studies on this platform, as it could offer a versatile approach for rapid vaccine development against novel pathogens.

2. **RSV Vaccination in Solid Organ Transplant Recipients: Interim Findings From a Phase 3 Trial of mRNA-1345.**

   Journal: Clin Infect Dis | Date: 2026 | Score: 15 (rule 12, llm +3 | Translation horizon: 0-12 months

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41711453/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41711453/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciag108](https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciag108)
   Type: Journal Article

   **Trial n:** n=150

   **Why it matters:**
   - Solid organ transplant recipients are highly vulnerable to severe respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) disease due to their chronic immunosuppression.
   - Current RSV prevention strategies are limited for this population, creating a significant unmet medical need.
   - A safe and effective RSV vaccine for transplant recipients could substantially reduce morbidity and mortality in this high-risk group.

   **Headline result:** The mRNA-1345 RSV vaccine was well tolerated and immunogenic in solid organ transplant recipients, with a single dose leading to 4.9-fold and 3.4-fold increases in RSV-A and RSV-B neutralizing antibody GMTs by Day 29, respectively.

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - Consider the mRNA-1345 vaccine for solid organ transplant recipients once approved, as it appears safe and immunogenic in this vulnerable population.
   - A single dose provides robust antibody responses, but a second dose may offer additional benefit, particularly for kidney and lung transplant recipients, those \<2 years post-transplant, or those on mycophenolate.
   - This is an interim analysis of an ongoing open-label Phase 3 trial, so long-term efficacy and safety data are still pending.

   **Read priority:** Read if time

3. **Safety and Immunogenicity of a Klebsiella pneumoniae Tetravalent Bioconjugate Vaccine (Kleb4V) Administered to Healthy Adults: A First-in-Human Phase I/II Randomized and Controlled Study.**

   Journal: J Infect Dis | Date: 2026 | Score: 14 (rule 12, llm +2 | Translation horizon: >12 months

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41289032/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41289032/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf600](https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf600)
   Type: Journal Article, Clinical Trial, Phase I, Randomized Controlled Trial

   **Trial n:** n=166

   **Why it matters:**
   - Klebsiella pneumoniae is a major cause of healthcare-associated infections, particularly in vulnerable populations, and is increasingly resistant to antibiotics.
   - There is an urgent global need for effective preventative strategies, including vaccines, to combat the rising burden of K. pneumoniae infections.
   - This study represents a critical early step in the development of a K. pneumoniae vaccine, potentially offering a new tool for infection control if successful in later phases.

   **Headline result:** The tetravalent bioconjugate vaccine Kleb4V was immunogenic and well tolerated in healthy adults aged 55-70 years, with AS03-adjuvanted formulations showing superior immune responses for three of four serotypes.

   - The superior immune response with AS03 adjuvant highlights its potential benefit, though further studies are required to confirm its role in clinical protection.
   - Responses for the O3b serotype were reduced compared to others, indicating a potential area for vaccine optimization in future development.

4. **Cabotegravir plus Rilpivirine for Persons with HIV and Adherence Challenges.**

   Journal: N Engl J Med | Date: 2026 | Score: 14 (rule 10, llm +4 | Translation horizon: 0-12 months

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41707171/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41707171/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2508228](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2508228)
   Type: Journal Article

   **Trial n:** n=306

   **Why it matters:**
   - Adherence to daily oral antiretroviral therapy (ART) remains a significant challenge for many persons with HIV, leading to virologic failure and drug resistance.
   - Long-acting injectable ART offers a potential solution to adherence barriers, but randomized trial evidence in populations with documented adherence challenges has been lacking.
   - This study provides crucial evidence supporting the use of long-acting injectables in a high-need population, potentially transforming care for those struggling with oral ART.

   **Headline result:** Monthly injections of long-acting cabotegravir-rilpivirine significantly reduced the cumulative incidence of regimen failure by week 48 to 22.8% compared to 41.2% with standard oral ART (difference, -18.4 percentage points; P = 0.002) in persons with HIV and adherence challenges.

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - Consider long-acting injectable cabotegravir-rilpivirine for persons with HIV who have documented adherence challenges to oral ART and achieve virologic suppression with support.
   - While effective, careful monitoring for virologic failure and resistance is still necessary, as resistance-associated mutations developed in a small number of participants in both groups.
   - The study population received adherence support and incentives prior to randomization, suggesting that a period of stabilization may be beneficial before initiating injectables.

   **Read priority:** Read now

5. **Immune Responses to BK Virus in Renal Transplant Recipients Receiving Intravenous Immunoglobulin Treatment.**

   Journal: J Infect Dis | Date: 2026 | Score: 12 (rule 10, llm +2 | Translation horizon: >12 months

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41071936/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41071936/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf525](https://doi.org/10.1093/infdis/jiaf525)
   Type: Journal Article, Randomized Controlled Trial, Multicenter Study

   **Trial n:** not reported

   **Why it matters:**
   - BK polyomavirus DNAemia is a common and serious complication in renal transplant recipients, increasing the risk of allograft failure.
   - Specific antiviral therapies for BKPyV are currently unavailable, leading to reliance on immunosuppression reduction, which carries its own risks.
   - Investigating host immune responses and potential adjunctive treatments like IVIG is critical for improving outcomes in this vulnerable patient population.

   **Headline result:** In this pilot study, 40% of IVIG recipients and 44.4% of placebo recipients cleared BK DNAemia at 3 months (relative risk, 0.90 \[95% CI, .23-2.89\]), indicating no clear impact of IVIG on viral clearance.

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - Two doses of IVIG (1 g/kg) for BK DNAemia in renal transplant recipients appear safe but did not demonstrate a clear benefit in viral clearance in this pilot study.
   - Viral clearance may be more dependent on intrinsic virus-specific host cellular and humoral responses, suggesting a need for further research into these mechanisms.
   - Given the lack of efficacy, IVIG should not be routinely used for BK DNAemia based on these findings, and immunosuppression reduction remains the primary strategy.

   **Read priority:** Awareness only

   Journal: mBio | Date: 2026 | Score: 12 (rule 9, llm +3 | Translation horizon: >12 months

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41718479/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41718479/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1128/mbio.03885-25](https://doi.org/10.1128/mbio.03885-25)
   Type: Journal Article

   **Trial n:** not reported

   **Why it matters:**
   - Candida glabrata is a major cause of invasive candidiasis, accounting for 15%-25% of cases, and is often difficult to treat due to intrinsic antifungal resistance.
   - The ability of C. glabrata to survive and proliferate within host macrophages contributes to immune evasion and persistent infections.
   - Understanding the molecular mechanisms governing C. glabrata's intracellular lifestyle is crucial for developing novel diagnostic and therapeutic strategies against this pathogen.

   **Headline result:** Candida glabrata actively delays its escape from macrophages, a process regulated by the Ksp1 kinase, with deletion of KSP1 accelerating macrophage lysis and increasing resistance to antifungals.

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - C. glabrata may actively prolong its intramacrophage phase, potentially contributing to immune evasion, antifungal resistance, and recurrent infections.
   - The identification of the Ksp1 kinase and its role in mitochondrial dysfunction and the formation of respiration-deficient variants (petites) highlights a potential target for novel antifungal strategies.
   - Clinicians should be aware that C. glabrata's intracellular survival and the generation of stress-resistant variants may necessitate adapted clinical diagnostics and therapy for persistent and recurrent fungal infections.

   Type: Journal Article

   - Rapid molecular assays are critical for timely diagnosis and appropriate treatment of methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) infections.
   - False-negative results from these assays can lead to delayed or incorrect antibiotic therapy, potentially worsening patient outcomes.
   - Understanding novel mechanisms of diagnostic evasion is crucial for maintaining the accuracy and reliability of rapid diagnostic tools.

   **Headline result:** CRISPR-Cas-associated SCCmec variants, found in 2% of 2432 screened S. aureus isolates, caused misdiagnosis in 11 of 40 SCCmec/junctions by BCID2 or Xpert assays, primarily restricted to clonal complex 5.

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - Be aware that rapid molecular MRSA diagnostic assays (e.g., Xpert, BCID2) can yield false-negative results due to novel SCCmec variants, particularly in hospital-associated clonal complex 5.
   - If clinical suspicion for MRSA remains high despite a negative rapid molecular test, consider conventional culture and susceptibility testing to avoid misdiagnosis and inappropriate treatment.
   - This finding highlights the need for ongoing surveillance of diagnostic assay performance and potential updates to primer designs to ensure continued accuracy.

9. **Tenecteplase versus standard medical treatment for basilar artery occlusion within 24 h (TRACE-5): a multicentre, prospective, randomised, open-label, blinded-endpoint, superiority, phase 3 trial.**

   Journal: Lancet | Date: 2026 | Score: 12 (rule 8, llm +4 | Translation horizon: 0-12 months

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41655588/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41655588/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(25)02633-9](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(25)02633-9)
   Type: Journal Article, Multicenter Study, Clinical Trial, Phase III

   **Trial n:** n=452

   **Why it matters:**
   - Basilar artery occlusion (BAO) is a severe form of ischemic stroke associated with high mortality and disability rates, posing a significant clinical challenge.
   - Current treatment options for BAO, especially beyond the standard 4.5-hour window for alteplase, are limited, often relying on endovascular thrombectomy which may not always be available or feasible.
   - Expanding the therapeutic window for intravenous thrombolysis could offer a crucial intervention for patients presenting later, potentially improving functional outcomes for a broader population.

   **Headline result:** Intravenous tenecteplase administered within 24 hours of symptom onset significantly improved functional outcome at 90 days, with 38% of patients achieving an mRS score of 0-1 or return to baseline, compared to 29% in the standard medical treatment group (adjusted relative rate 1.50, 95% CI 1.09-2.08, p=0.014).

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - Consider tenecteplase (0.25 mg/kg, max 25 mg) for patients with basilar artery occlusion presenting within 24 hours of symptom onset, particularly when endovascular thrombectomy is delayed or unavailable.
   - While symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage rates were similar, this study was conducted in China, and generalizability to diverse populations and healthcare systems should be considered.
   - Tenecteplase appears to be a safe and effective option, but its optimal role in conjunction with or as an alternative to endovascular thrombectomy requires further clarification.

   - This is a small, multicenter case series; larger, ideally randomized, studies are needed to confirm efficacy, optimal dosing, and safety profile.
