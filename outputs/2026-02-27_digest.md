1. **Intestinal Mucosal Immune Responses to Novel Oral Poliovirus Vaccine Type 2 in Healthy Newborns.**

   Journal: Clin Infect Dis | Date: 2026 | Score: 15 (rule 12, llm +3 | Translation horizon: 0-12 months

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40907970/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40907970/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciaf484](https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciaf484)
   Type: Journal Article

   **Trial n:** n=215 healthy newborns (110 nOPV2, 105 placebo)

   **Why it matters:**
   - Circulating vaccine-derived poliovirus type 2 (cVDPV2) outbreaks pose a significant threat to polio eradication efforts globally.
   - Novel oral poliovirus vaccine type 2 (nOPV2) is a critical tool for outbreak response, with billions of doses already administered.
   - Understanding the immune response, particularly intestinal mucosal immunity, in newborns is vital for optimizing vaccination strategies in vulnerable populations.

   **Headline result:** Two doses of nOPV2 in healthy newborns induced strong intestinal mucosal antibody responses, with 90.0% showing positive PV2-specific neutralization in stool at 8 weeks, significantly different from placebo (P \< .0001).

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - nOPV2 is effective in inducing robust intestinal mucosal immunity in newborns, supporting its use from birth in cVDPV2 outbreak settings to enhance population-level protection.
   - The strong response after the second dose, even in those without a first-dose response, highlights the importance of completing the 2-dose series for optimal immunity in neonates.
   - This study focused on immune response, and further research on clinical efficacy in preventing infection and transmission in real-world outbreak settings is still valuable.

2. **Adjunctive Fosfomycin for the Treatment of Staphylococcus aureus Bacteremia: A Pooled Post Hoc Analysis of Individual Participant Data From 2 Randomized Trials.**

   Journal: Clin Infect Dis | Date: 2026 | Score: 15 (rule 12, llm +3 | Translation horizon: 0-12 months

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40670317/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40670317/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciaf387](https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciaf387)
   Type: Journal Article

   **Trial n:** n=369

   **Why it matters:**
   - Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia (SAB) is a severe infection with high morbidity and mortality, and optimal treatment strategies, especially for adjunctive therapies, remain uncertain.
   - Adjunctive therapies are often explored to improve outcomes in SAB, particularly for difficult-to-treat cases or those with persistent bacteremia.
   - Understanding the risk-benefit profile of adding agents like fosfomycin is crucial for guiding clinical decisions and potentially improving patient outcomes in SAB management.

   **Headline result:** Adjunctive fosfomycin therapy showed a 91.8% posterior probability of improving treatment success at 8 weeks (median relative risk, 1.10) and significantly reduced persistent bacteremia at day 3 (RR, 0.19) and day 7 (RR, 0.22), but was associated with a higher risk of adverse events (RR, 2.03).

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - Consider adjunctive fosfomycin in severe S. aureus bacteremia, particularly when early bacterial clearance is critical, but be vigilant for increased adverse events.
   - The benefit in overall treatment success at 8 weeks was modest (RR 1.10), and the increased toxicity (RR 2.03 for adverse events) requires careful patient selection and monitoring.
   - This pooled analysis provides stronger evidence for fosfomycin's role, but individual patient factors and local resistance patterns should guide its use.

   **Read priority:** Read if time

3. **Reducing Household Tuberculosis Transmission: A Pilot Cluster-Randomized Controlled Trial.**

   Journal: Clin Infect Dis | Date: 2026 | Score: 14 (rule 12, llm +2 | Translation horizon: >12 months

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41003685/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41003685/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciaf526](https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciaf526)
   Type: Journal Article

   **Trial n:** 179 household contacts with negative baseline QFT completed follow-up

   **Why it matters:**
   - Tuberculosis (TB) remains a significant global health challenge, with household transmission being a major driver of new infections.
   - Understanding effective strategies to reduce TB transmission within households is crucial for public health efforts, especially in high-burden settings.
   - The duration of infectiousness after treatment initiation is uncertain, making targeted interventions for household contacts difficult to optimize.

   **Headline result:** A bundled intervention did not significantly reduce new TB infections in household contacts, with an incidence risk ratio of 1.10 (95% CI 0.71-1.71) compared to standard care.

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - Current evidence suggests that intensive household isolation and restrictive measures for TB patients after treatment initiation may not be an effective strategy for preventing new infections in household contacts.
   - Adherence to the bundled intervention was moderate (~53-54%), which could have limited its effectiveness, suggesting challenges in implementing such measures in real-world settings.
   - Focus should remain on early diagnosis and effective treatment of index cases, and preventive therapy for high-risk contacts, rather than relying on behavioral isolation measures.

   **Read priority:** Awareness only

6. **Cabotegravir plus Rilpivirine for Persons with HIV and Adherence Challenges.**

   Journal: N Engl J Med | Date: 2026 | Score: 14 (rule 10, llm +4 | Translation horizon: 0-12 months

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41707171/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41707171/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2508228](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2508228)
   Type: Journal Article

   **Trial n:** n=306

   **Why it matters:**
   - Adherence to daily oral antiretroviral therapy (ART) remains a significant challenge for many persons with HIV, leading to virologic failure and drug resistance.
   - Long-acting injectable ART offers a potential solution to adherence barriers, but randomized trial evidence in populations with documented adherence challenges has been lacking.
   - This study provides crucial evidence supporting the use of long-acting injectables in a high-need population, potentially transforming care for those struggling with oral ART.

   **Headline result:** Monthly injections of long-acting cabotegravir-rilpivirine significantly reduced the cumulative incidence of regimen failure by week 48 to 22.8% compared to 41.2% with standard oral ART (difference, -18.4 percentage points; P = 0.002) in persons with HIV and adherence challenges.

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - Consider long-acting injectable cabotegravir-rilpivirine for persons with HIV who have documented adherence challenges to oral ART and achieve virologic suppression with support.
   - While effective, careful monitoring for virologic failure and resistance is still necessary, as resistance-associated mutations developed in a small number of participants in both groups.
   - The study population received adherence support and incentives prior to randomization, suggesting that a period of stabilization may be beneficial before initiating injectables.

7. **Tecovirimat for the Treatment of Mpox.**

   Journal: N Engl J Med | Date: 2026 | Score: 14 (rule 9, llm +5 | Translation horizon: 0-12 months

   PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41740032/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41740032/)

   DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2506495](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2506495)
   Type: Journal Article

   **Trial n:** n=412 randomized, 336 in primary analysis

   **Why it matters:**
   - Mpox caused a global outbreak, highlighting the urgent need for effective antiviral treatments, especially for severe cases or immunocompromised individuals.
   - Tecovirimat was widely used during the mpox outbreak based on animal data and compassionate use, but robust human efficacy data were lacking.
   - Understanding the true clinical benefit of tecovirimat is crucial for guiding treatment decisions and resource allocation in future outbreaks.

   **Headline result:** Tecovirimat did not significantly shorten the time to clinical resolution (estimated cumulative incidence 83% with tecovirimat vs 84% with placebo; HR 0.98, 95% CI 0.74-1.31) or reduce pain in adults with clade II mpox.

   **Clinical takeaway:**
   - Do not routinely use tecovirimat for mild to moderate clade II mpox, as this trial showed no benefit in time to clinical resolution or pain reduction.
   - Consider tecovirimat only for severe mpox, immunocompromised patients, or those with lesions in sensitive areas, acknowledging that efficacy in these specific subgroups was not demonstrated in this study.
   - This study focused on clade II mpox; efficacy against other clades or in specific high-risk populations (e.g., severe disease, immunocompromised) still requires further investigation.

   **Read priority:** Read now

   Type: Journal Article

   **Trial n:** n=129

   **Why it matters:**

   - Letermovir prophylaxis has been effective in preventing CMV reactivation, but understanding immune recovery is key for guiding post-prophylaxis management.
   - A reliable biomarker for CMV-specific cell-mediated immunity (CMV-CMI) could help identify patients at highest risk for post-prophylaxis CMV infection and tailor surveillance strategies.

   - Continue to monitor HCT recipients for CMV reactivation post-prophylaxis based on established protocols, regardless of CMV-CMI assay results.
   - Further research is needed to identify more reliable immune biomarkers for guiding CMV management after HCT.
